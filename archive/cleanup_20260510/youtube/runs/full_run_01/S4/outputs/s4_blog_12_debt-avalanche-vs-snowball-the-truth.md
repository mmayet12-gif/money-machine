### The Truth About Debt Avalance vs Snowball

When it comes to paying off debt, there are two popular strategies that often come up in discussions: the debt avalanche and snowball methods. While mathematically, one method might seem superior, behavior plays a significant role in determining which strategy is best for you.

In this article, we'll break down both methods, provide practical examples, and offer guidance on how to choose the right approach for your financial situation.

#### What's the Debt Avalanche Method?

The debt avalanche involves paying off debts with the highest interest rates first, regardless of their balance. This means if you have multiple credit cards with different interest rates, you'll focus on paying off the one with the highest rate first.

**Example:** Let's say you have two credit cards: Card A with a $2,000 balance and 20% interest rate, and Card B with a $1,500 balance but only 10% interest rate. With the debt avalanche method, you'd pay as much as possible towards Card A until it's paid off.

#### What's the Debt Snowball Method?

The debt snowball involves paying off debts with the smallest balances first, while still making minimum payments on other debts. This approach can provide a psychological boost by quickly eliminating smaller debts and creating momentum in your debt repayment journey.

**Example:** Continuing from our previous example, if you choose the debt snowball method, you'd focus on paying off Card B first since it has the smallest balance. Once Card B is paid off, you'd then move on to Card A.

#### The Math: Why Debt Avalanche Often Wins

From a purely mathematical standpoint, the debt avalanche method makes sense. By paying off debts with the highest interest rates first, you'll save more money in interest over time and become debt-free faster.

Let's say your credit card has an $1,000 balance and an 18% interest rate. If you only pay the minimum payment each month ($25), it will take you 5 years to pay off the debt, with a total cost of $2,342 (including interest).

Now, if you apply an extra $100 towards the principal every month, you'll save over $1,300 in interest and become debt-free in just 3.5 years.

#### But Behavior Matters: Why Debt Snowball Often Wins

While mathematically sound, the debt avalanche method often falls short in real-world scenarios due to human behavior. Paying off smaller debts first can provide a sense of accomplishment and momentum, encouraging you to continue tackling your debt.

Research has shown that small wins can lead to significant motivation boosts, which is essential for sustained financial progress. By quickly eliminating smaller debts, you'll experience a psychological snowball effect, making it easier to tackle larger debts.

#### The Decision Guide

So, how do you choose between the debt avalanche and snowball methods?

1.  **Assess your debts:** List all your debts with their balances and interest rates.
2.  **Consider your behavior:** Think about what motivates you and whether you're more likely to stick with a strategy that provides quick wins or saves money in interest.
3.  **Start small:** Begin with either method, and adjust as needed based on your progress.

### FAQ

**Q: Is one method better than the other?**
A: Both methods have their merits, but mathematically, the debt avalanche method is often more effective.

**Q: Can I combine both methods?**
A: Yes, you can alternate between paying off high-interest debts and smaller balances to balance mathematical efficiency with psychological motivation.
