**(0:16-3:20) Context and Urgency**
Let's start with the basics. If you have a high-deductible health plan, you're eligible to open a Health Savings Account, or HSA for short. It's like a 401(k) for healthcare expenses. But unlike other investment accounts, an HSA offers triple tax advantages: contributions are tax-deductible, growth is tax-free, and withdrawals for qualified medical expenses are tax-free.

[VISUAL: An animation of a person adding money to their HSA]

**(3:21-6:45) Act 1: Eligibility**
So, who can use an HSA? If you have a high-deductible health plan  that's a plan with a minimum deductible of $1,400 for individuals and $2,800 for families  you're eligible. It doesn't matter if you're single or married, as long as your employer offers the plan. And it's not just about medical expenses; an HSA can also be used to pay for some dependent care costs.

So, what can you do with an HSA besides covering medical expenses? You can invest it. And here's the kicker: any leftover funds in your HSA after age 65 are yours to keep, tax-free. That's right; you don't have to use them for healthcare expenses. You can use them as a nest egg for retirement.
